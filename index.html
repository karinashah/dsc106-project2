Proposition: "States with fewer abortion clinics have higher rates of residents traveling out-of-state for abortion care."

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Added a regression trendline to suggest strong correlation, even if outliers exist. -0.5
  • Used color encoding (% of counties without a clinic) to amplify emotional response about access issues. +1
  • Bubble size encoding (% of women without local clinic) emphasizes bigger states, making the situation feel more dramatic. -0.5

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Used reversed and ambiguous color gradient (plasma) to blur regional differences. -1.5
  • Removed trendlines or annotations to make interpretations harder. -1
  • Color associations don’t match viewers’ expectations, making patterns less intuitive. -2

Final Reflection

This project taught me that data visualization is not neutral — even when using accurate data, the design process inherently involves choices that shape what viewers see and feel. Through this assignment, I discovered how subtle design shifts — like changing a color gradient or including a regression line — can dramatically alter a viewer’s perception of causality or urgency. I also realized that deception isn't always obvious: emotional emphasis (like bubble size or red zones) can steer readers subtly, even if no data is “falsified.” The line between ethical persuasion and deception lies in intent and clarity. Earnest visualizations aim to reveal truth through clear design. Deceptive ones might hide or distort patterns to protect an argument. I learned that persuasive design isn't about making visuals flashy — it’s about framing narratives carefully. The most interesting challenge was designing the "AGAINST" visualization: it forced me to weaken a real pattern without lying, and that subtlety was surprisingly powerful. Going forward, I’ll be more critical of how I design — and how I interpret — every visualization I see.
